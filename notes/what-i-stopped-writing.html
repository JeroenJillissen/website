What I stopped writing myself

Every week I used to spend twenty minutes writing a standup update. What I worked on, what's blocked, what's next. Useful for the team, tedious to produce. The information existed in my head, in Slack threads, in Notion comments — I just had to assemble it.

Now I paste those sources into a prompt and get a draft in thirty seconds. I spend two minutes editing it. The update is better than what I used to write because it's less influenced by whatever was on my mind when I sat down to type.

This keeps coming up when I talk to designers about AI. They expect the gains to be in the actual design work — generating UI, exploring ideas. And those gains are real. But the hours I've saved are mostly in the work around the work. Updating stakeholders. Documenting decisions. Synthesising research into a format someone will actually read.

None of that feels glamorous, which is probably why people don't talk about it much. But it's where the time actually goes.
